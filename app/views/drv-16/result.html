What's {{tenure}}? {{#tenure_caution}}

A caution against first registration protects land or property which hasn’t yet been registered with the Land Registry. It entitles the person who registered the caution to be told if someone tries to register the property, and object to that request if it conflicts with their interest in the property.

More about cautions against first registration {{/tenure_caution}} {{#tenure_leasehold}}

You only own a leasehold property for a fixed period of time. A legal agreement with the person who owns the building and land it stands on (known as the ‘freeholder’) will tell you how many years you’ll own the property. Most flats are leasehold. Houses can be leasehold too and usually are if they’re bought through a shared ownership scheme. The leasehold and freehold are each registered separately with the Land Registry and have their own title documents. You are buying the summary for the current leaseholder, not the freeholder.

More about Leasehold on GOV.UK {{/tenure_leasehold}} {{#tenure_freehold}}

You own a freehold building and the land it stands on outright. It is yours until you sell it or give it away. Most houses are freehold. Most flats are leasehold and someone else owns the freehold for the building the flat is in. If you want to know who owns a particular flat, you probably need the leasehold instead. Houses can also be leasehold and usually are if they’re bought through a shared ownership scheme.
